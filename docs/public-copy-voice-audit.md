# Public Copy Voice Audit

Issue: 2-0 - Recon public copy voice, clarity, and positioning

Scope reviewed:

- `/`
- `/about`
- `/projects`
- `/services`
- `/contact`

This is a recon document only. It does not rewrite live site copy.

## Core Question

Can a visitor quickly understand that CurlTech helps people clean up messy work
and turn it into simple systems that are easier to run?

Partly. The current copy supports the idea, but it often says it in a polished,
consulting-adjacent way. The best lines already point toward messy handoffs,
trusted records, unclear workflows, and small practical improvements. The gap is
that the site does not yet say the plain version early enough or often enough:

> CurlTech helps small teams turn messy work into simple systems that are easier
> to run, trust, and improve.

The current copy is credible and controlled, but it sometimes feels written for
an internal strategy memo instead of a small-business owner or operations lead
trying to explain a real mess in 20 seconds.

## Site-Wide Assessment

### Current Voice

The voice is careful, disciplined, and credible. It avoids hype and does not
sound like a startup landing page. That is a strength.

The weakness is that the voice sometimes gets too abstract. Phrases like
"operational systems," "delivery discipline," "operating foundation," and
"capability demonstrations" are accurate, but they do not always sound like a
real operator talking through a problem at a table.

Current voice grade: `B-`

### Current Clarity

Most sentences are understandable after one read, but the site leans on repeated
abstract nouns: workflows, systems, records, operations, modernization,
structure, clarity, coordination. Those words are useful, but they need more
real-world anchors.

The clearest copy appears when the site names common problems directly:
handoffs, intake, tracking, records, coordination, status visibility, brittle
steps, and manual tracking.

Current clarity grade: `B-`

### Current Persuasiveness

The site makes the value of CurlTech visible, but not yet sharp. A visitor can
tell CurlTech works on operational software and workflow cleanup. They may not
immediately know whether their problem fits unless they keep reading.

The site needs a stronger first-screen promise and more customer pain language:
"we are tracking this in too many places," "no one trusts the status," "the
handoff breaks," "the old tool works until it does not," "the work depends on
one person remembering the process."

Current persuasiveness grade: `C+`

### Current Estimated Reading Level

Estimated reading level: around 10th to 11th grade.

The words are not difficult by themselves, but the noun density raises the
reading level. The site would feel closer to 9th grade if more lines used
concrete nouns and active verbs:

- "messy handoffs" instead of "operational conditions"
- "work gets stuck" instead of "workflow bottlenecks"
- "records people can trust" instead of "process integrity"
- "small changes people can verify" instead of "disciplined implementation"

### Does It Sound Like a Real Person?

Sometimes. The best lines sound practical and human:

- "Messy handoffs need structure"
- "Bring the process that feels harder than it should"
- "Start with the workflow, then choose the smallest useful move"

Other lines sound more like positioning copy:

- "Operational capability demonstrations"
- "Focused support for the parts of the operation that need structure"
- "Veteran-owned systems work for real operational conditions"

### Does It Sound Like a Generic Consultant?

Not fully, but it risks that impression in places. The site is more disciplined
than a generic consultant site, yet it still uses phrases many firms could use:

- "practical modernization"
- "workflow clarity"
- "operational visibility"
- "reliable records"
- "disciplined implementation"
- "clearer workflows"

These phrases are not wrong. They need sharper proof, examples, and contrast.

### Repeated Weak Phrases

- operational systems
- practical modernization
- workflow clarity
- reliable records
- disciplined implementation
- operational visibility
- clearer structure
- useful next step
- operating foundation
- real operational conditions

These phrases can stay in the vocabulary, but they should not carry the whole
message.

### Phrases Worth Keeping

- "Messy handoffs need structure"
- "records are hard to trust"
- "tools do not match the way the work actually moves"
- "the process that feels harder than it should"
- "what the work is, where it slows down"
- "smallest useful move"
- "software people can run"
- "without adding unnecessary process weight"

These sound closest to CurlTech: plain, operational, and grounded in real work.

### Missing Customer Pain Points

- Work is tracked in spreadsheets, email, memory, and side conversations.
- People cannot tell what is current.
- Handoffs depend on one person knowing the whole process.
- Intake is inconsistent.
- Status is unclear.
- Records exist, but no one fully trusts them.
- Old tools still run the business but are painful to change.
- The team needs help improving the system without disrupting daily work.

### Missing Trust Signals

- Clear explanation of how an engagement starts and what the first output is.
- More specific examples of past work outcomes without overstating them.
- A stronger link between project proof and the services offered.
- A plain statement that CurlTech favors small, verifiable changes over big
  vague transformations.
- More visible evidence that the work is shaped by operational reality, not just
  software craft.

### Missing Simple Explanation of What CurlTech Does

The site needs one plain sentence that can work across the homepage, services,
and contact page:

> CurlTech helps small teams clean up messy workflows and turn them into simple
> software systems people can run, trust, and improve.

## Route-by-Route Grading

## `/`

Overall grade: `B-`

What the page is trying to do:

The homepage introduces CurlTech as an operational systems practice, summarizes
capabilities, shows proof points, gives a short about snapshot, and points
visitors toward contact or services.

Whether it succeeds:

Mostly. It gives a credible picture of the work and avoids hype. The page is
usable and directionally right. The gap is that the top message still sounds
more like a consultant category than a plain customer problem.

Best existing line:

> Operational clarity, turned into software people can run.

Weakest existing line:

> Operational systems built for work that has to hold up.

That line is not bad, but it is broad. It asks the visitor to infer what kind of
work, what problem, and what improvement.

What sounds least like CurlTech:

"Capability Summary" and "Selected Systems" feel more formal than the strongest
operator voice elsewhere on the page.

First thing to rewrite later:

The hero headline and lede.

Rewrite direction, not a full rewrite:

Lead with messy work and practical systems. Name the customer problem first:
unclear handoffs, records people do not trust, tools that do not match the way
the work actually happens. Then explain that CurlTech turns that into simple,
verifiable systems.

## `/about`

Overall grade: `B`

What the page is trying to do:

The about page explains Greg Curl / CurlTech credibility, working principles,
and the operational philosophy behind the work.

Whether it succeeds:

Yes, more than the other pages. It has some of the strongest and most human copy
on the site. It explains that operational work often breaks because handoffs,
records, and tools do not line up.

Best existing line:

> Operational work rarely fails because a team lacks effort.

Weakest existing line:

> Veteran-owned systems work for real operational conditions.

The thought is valid, but "real operational conditions" is vague. It sounds
like a positioning phrase rather than a real person naming a work problem.

What sounds least like CurlTech:

"Operational Credibility" as a label. The section itself is strong, but the
label is stiff.

First thing to rewrite later:

The top headline.

Rewrite direction, not a full rewrite:

Keep the credibility, but reduce biography-first framing. Make the page about
how CurlTech thinks about broken work: messy handoffs, untrusted records, and
systems that need to survive normal use.

## `/projects`

Overall grade: `C`

What the page is trying to do:

The projects page shows selected systems as proof of CurlTech's approach.

Whether it succeeds:

Partly. The projects are relevant, but the copy is too compressed and abstract.
It names categories of proof but does not yet show enough real-world problem,
constraint, or practical result for a visitor to remember.

Best existing line:

> Proof through practical operating problems, not inflated claims.

Weakest existing line:

> Operational capability demonstrations.

That headline is accurate but cold. A small-business or operations-minded
visitor is unlikely to say it out loud or remember it.

What sounds least like CurlTech:

"Human-centered coordination," "field and workflow systems," and "public
platform delivery" are generic category labels. They do not yet carry enough
specific operational meaning.

First thing to rewrite later:

Each project description.

Rewrite direction, not a full rewrite:

Make every project follow a simple proof pattern: the messy work, what the
system made clearer, and why that matters. Avoid turning the page into a case
study if the proof is not ready, but give each project one concrete operational
problem.

## `/services`

Overall grade: `B-`

What the page is trying to do:

The services page tells visitors what kinds of help CurlTech offers and how an
engagement starts.

Whether it succeeds:

Mostly. This page has useful structure and clear service areas. It names the
right problems, but the service labels still sound like consulting categories.
The "How It Starts" section is strong because it explains the first step in
plain terms.

Best existing line:

> Start with the workflow, then choose the smallest useful move.

Weakest existing line:

> Focused support for the parts of the operation that need structure.

That line is understandable, but soft. It does not create a vivid reason to
reach out.

What sounds least like CurlTech:

"Records and process integrity" sounds formal compared with the more human
"records people can trust."

First thing to rewrite later:

Service area headings and descriptions.

Rewrite direction, not a full rewrite:

Translate service labels into customer situations. For example: "When the work
is tracked in too many places," "When handoffs break," "When old tools are
still running the business," and "When the next step needs to be scoped before
it is built."

## `/contact`

Overall grade: `B`

What the page is trying to do:

The contact page tells visitors when to reach out and what to bring to the first
conversation.

Whether it succeeds:

Yes. It is short, practical, and more human than most of the site. It makes the
first step feel low-friction. The main weakness is that the headline is cleverer
than it is clear.

Best existing line:

> Bring the process that feels harder than it should: handoffs, intake,
> tracking, records, coordination, or status visibility.

Weakest existing line:

> Start a conversation about the work behind the work.

It has rhythm, but it is less clear than the lines below it. A visitor may not
know what "work behind the work" means until they read further.

What sounds least like CurlTech:

"work behind the work" is memorable but a little vague. CurlTech should sound
plain first, clever second.

First thing to rewrite later:

The headline.

Rewrite direction, not a full rewrite:

Make the call to action about a stuck or messy workflow. Keep the helpful
prompt about what to bring, because it already lowers the barrier to contact.

## Strongest Current Copy

The strongest current copy is on `/contact` and `/about`.

Strongest line:

> Bring the process that feels harder than it should: handoffs, intake,
> tracking, records, coordination, or status visibility.

Why it works:

It is concrete, easy to understand, and customer-centered. It names real work
problems without sounding dramatic or salesy.

## Weakest Current Copy

Weakest line:

> Operational capability demonstrations.

Why it is weak:

It sounds abstract and internal. It does not explain what the visitor will see
or why it matters. It also does not sound like a phrase a practical operator
would use in conversation.

## Messaging North Star

Recommended mantra:

> Simple systems for messy work.

Why this is strongest:

It is short, plain-spoken, memorable, and flexible. It creates immediate
contrast between the customer's problem and CurlTech's value. It is easy to say
out loud, easy to explain in 20 seconds, and broad enough to cover workflow
clarity, records, modernization, and small-business systems without sounding
corporate.

### 20-Second Explanation

CurlTech helps small teams clean up messy workflows and turn them into simple
systems people can actually run. That might mean clearer intake, better
handoffs, records people can trust, or replacing brittle manual steps with
software that fits the way the work really happens.

### Who CurlTech Helps

CurlTech helps small businesses, service teams, and internal operations where
important work depends on messy handoffs, scattered tracking, unclear status, or
tools that no longer match the job.

### What Problem CurlTech Solves

CurlTech solves the problem of work that has become too hard to see, trust, or
manage because the process lives across memory, spreadsheets, email, old tools,
and informal workarounds.

### What Changes After CurlTech Helps

The work becomes easier to explain, easier to run, and easier to improve. People
know what enters the system, who touches it, where the record lives, what status
means, and what the next useful step should be.

### Five Possible Mantras

1. Simple systems for messy work.
2. Make messy work easier to run.
3. Clear systems for real work.
4. Turn workarounds into systems.
5. Clean up the work. Build what helps.

Recommended strongest mantra: `Simple systems for messy work.`

## Follow-On Rewrite Issue Recommendations

### 1. Rewrite Homepage Hero and Primary Positioning

Page or area affected: `/`

Why it matters:

The homepage needs the clearest possible answer to what CurlTech does and why a
visitor should care. The current hero is credible but too abstract.

Expected outcome:

A stronger hero headline, lede, and first CTA area that make the messy-work to
simple-system promise obvious within a few seconds.

### 2. Rewrite Services Around Customer Situations

Page or area affected: `/services`

Why it matters:

Visitors need to recognize their own problem before they care about service
categories. The current service areas are accurate but could sound like many
consulting sites.

Expected outcome:

Service copy organized around real operational pain points: broken handoffs,
scattered tracking, untrusted records, brittle old tools, and scoped first
moves.

### 3. Rewrite Projects as Practical Proof Points

Page or area affected: `/projects`

Why it matters:

The projects page should prove the positioning. Right now it lists relevant
systems, but it does not yet make the operational problem and useful change
memorable enough.

Expected outcome:

Each project gets a tighter proof pattern: problem, system direction, and what
became clearer or easier to run.

## Rewrite Direction Summary

Future page rewrites should:

- Lead with the customer's messy work, not CurlTech's category.
- Use fewer abstract nouns per sentence.
- Say "records people can trust" more often than "process integrity."
- Say "messy handoffs" more often than "operational conditions."
- Show how the first engagement starts.
- Keep the practical, disciplined tone.
- Avoid hype, inflated proof, and generic transformation language.
- Make every page easy to explain in a 20-second conversation.
